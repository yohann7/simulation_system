Finite Element Simulation and Parameter Optimization
of SWRH82B Wire Rod in Stelmor Cooling Process
T.W. Deng, F. Cui, Z.Y. Tang, D.D. Cao, W. Tang, and M. Zeng
Submitted: 28 March 2024 / Revised: 2 July 2024 / Accepted: 21 July 2024 / Published online: 6 August 2024
Based on the ﬁeld production conditions, the air cooling process for SWRH82B in Stelmor was designed
and simulated. The temperature variations of the surface and core of both the lap region and non lap regionof the wire rod were measured under different cooling conditions, and the discrepancy in cooling betweenthe surface and core was analyzed. A reasonable ﬁnite element model was developed and evaluated using
ANSYS ﬁnite element analysis software to study the temperature ﬁeld changes of the SWRH82B wire rod
during controlled cooling. The node temperature drop curve and transient temperature ﬁeld distribution oftheU12.5 mm SWRH82B wire rod during the 880 /C176C laying process were determined. By adjusting the
Optiﬂex device angle and coil spacing, similar cooling rates for the lapped and non lapped sections were
achieved, minimizing temperature differences and improving temperature uniformity. Simulation results
indicate that maintaining a 3 /C176opening angle for the Optiﬂex device of the ﬁrst four blowers and a 1.5 /C176
opening angle for the remaining four blowers resulted in comparable surface temperatures for the wire rodin both lapped and non lapped areas during the air cooling process, with a temperature difference of up to
6/C176C. As the initial roller speed increased, the temperature difference on the surface of each wire rod in the
lap area ﬁrstly decreases and then rise again, reaching a minimum between 0.70 and 0.75 m/s, with acorresponding wire rod spacing between 73.90 and 79.18 mm. Optimization simulation of the laying
temperature at 880 /C176C, demonstrated a SWRH82B microstructure with a 92% sorbite occupancy rate,
network cementite below 1 grade, and core martensite less than 0.5 grade.
Keywords controlled cooling, ﬁnite element simulation,
SWRH82B, stelmor, uniform cooling
1. Introduction
SWRH82B wire rod plays a crucial role as a high carbon
steel in the manufacturing of high-strength, large-scale pre-stressed steel strands and high-strength, low-relaxation pre-
stressed steel wires. It is found extensive applications in public
infrastructure, energy, and marine engineering sectors (Ref 1-
3). Despite its signiﬁcance, the SWRH82B wire rod produced
in China faces several challenges, including structural irregu-
larities, lower sorbite rate, and the presence of martensite andsecondary cementite in the core of the wire rod. The production
process of steel wire for bridge cables using SWRH82B
involves several steps. First, the steel billets are hot rolled withan initial rolling temperature of 1120-1150 /C176C and a ﬁnishing
rolling temperature of 900-950 /C176C. The wire rod is formed ataround 900 /C176C. After hot rolling, the steel is coiled into a form
known as a wire rod. Following this, the wire rod undergoes
rapid cooling from the austenitizing temperature to below the
pearlite transformation temperature for isothermal transforma-tion to achieve a ﬁne, fully pearlitic microstructure (Ref 4). This
process is referred to as sorbitizing treatment. Typically,
increasing the degree of supercooling can reﬁne the interlamel-
lar spacing of pearlite and increase the sorbite content, therebyenhancing the mechanical properties of the wire rod (Ref 5).
The wire rod is subsequently drawn into steel wire or steel
strand, pearlitic steel can obtain extremely high tensile strengthafter severe plastic deformation (Ref 6-8). Before drawing, the
wire rod undergoes pickling and phosphating (Ref 9), followed
by continuous multi-pass cold drawing to produce the steelwire, which is then hot-dip galvanized in a zinc bath at around
450/C176C (Ref 10). To reduce creep, decrease stress relaxation,
and improve the stability of the steel wire, a stabilizingtreatment is performed after galvanizing. The tensile strength ofhigh-quality steel wire can reach the level of 1960 MPa.
In practical production, sorbitizing treatment can be cate-
gorized into two types: in-line quenching (post-rolling residualheat quenching) and reheat quenching. Reheat quenching,
primarily lead bath quenching (lead patenting, LP), is seldom
used due to its large footprint, high energy consumption, lowproductivity, and high production cost. In-line quenching
involves placing the quenching equipment at the end of the
rolling line to use the ﬁnishing rolling temperature forquenching the wire rod. Currently, in-line quenching methodsfor wire rods include the Stelmor controlled cooling process
line(direct patenting, DP), post-rolling water quenching line
(Ref 11), and salt bath quenching controlled cooling process

Stelmor controlled cooling process line is the most mature andwidely applied technology.
There is a growing need to investigate the controlled rolling
and controlled cooling processes of SWRH82B wire rod. The
controlled cooling process signiﬁcantly impacts the microstruc-ture and mechanical properties of the wire rod (Ref 13-15).
Through the strategic control of cooling conditions, it is
possible to substantially enhance the overall mechanicalproperties and service life of the material. Controlled cooling
is primarily achieved through two means: regulating the laying
temperature of the wire after water cooling and controlling thecooling rate of the wire on the air-cooled roller to achieve ahigher sorbite rate. The high carbon steel SWRH82B hot rolled
wire rod undergoes cooling primarily on the Stelmor air cooling
line. Following the passage of the hot rolled wire rod throughthe enhanced water cooling box, the leading end on the roller
conveyor forms a circular loop metal wire ring. Subsequently,
forced air cooling is employed through a series of blowersbeneath the roller conveyor to attain the desired microstructure
and properties (Ref 16). In this study, the laying temperature of
SWRH82B wire rod was set at approximately 880 /C176C.
The dynamic continuous cooling transformation curves of
steel are commonly used to guide the controlled rolling and
cooling process (Ref 17). Austenite deformation is utilized to
mimic hot rolling deformation and is also employed in thesubsequent cooling process to manage the microstructure and
properties of the steel (Ref 18-20). Currently, the impact of
controlled cooling parameters on the microstructure andproperties of SWRH82B steel has been investigated (Ref 14,
21-23), however, there is a lack of simulation studies on the
factory production site, and the process of SWRH82B wire rodcooling in Stelmor air cooling still requires further enhance-
ment and investigation. Exploring and testing SWRH82B wire
rods of various speciﬁcations on the production line would belabor-intensive and resource-consuming. We studied the solid-
state phase transformation law of SWRH82B and used the ﬁnite
element numerical simulation method to set accurate boundaryconditions in combination with on-site production data. Reli-able temperature data could be calculated to provide an
effective solution for temperature control during wire controlled
cooling.
The Stelmor cooling process effectively controls the cooling
rate and phase transformation of the wire ring by adjusting the
blower opening and air velocity distribution within the wirering, ultimately optimizing the microstructure and mechanical
properties of the wire rod (Ref 24). Upon the wire being
dispersed onto the conveyor by the laying machine, overlap-ping and non-overlapping points appear on the cross-section ofthe conveyor, leading to an unequal wire density distribution.
At the overlap points, the wire density is highest, while at the
center, the wire density is lowest. The intricate geometricmicrostructure of the metal wire ring results in varying cooling
rates across different areas. This non-uniform wire density
distribution causes slower cooling rates at overlapping pointscompared to other positions, consequently causing a lag in the
phase transition starting point and time at varying degrees (Ref
25-28).
In this study, ANSYS simulation software was used to
conduct ﬁnite element analysis of process parameters during
controlled cooling of SWRH82B steel. By integrating the phasetransformation patterns of SWRH82B steel with cooling ratesderived from on-site temperature measurements, an innovativephase transformation model was developed. This research
provides highly reliable guidance for the production of high-speed wire rods.

2.3 Stelmor Air Cooling Simulation Process
The Stelmor air cooling simulation process involves various
parameters inﬂuencing the cooling rate of the wire, such as wirespeed, wire diameter, conveyor speed, air ﬂow rate, and ﬁlling
density. The discrepancy between the wire speed and conveyor
speed can result in changes in the ring spacing, therebyimpacting the wire’s cooling rate. Increasing the ring spacing
can reduce the bulk density to enhance heat transfer. The main
objectives of simulating the air cooling process revolve arounddetermining the optimal opening angle of the Optiﬂex device toachieve uniform cooling rates for the lapped and non lapped
sections on the air cooling line and establishing an appropriate
wire rod spacing post-laying to minimize temperature differ-entials in the lap area. These goals aim to ensure uniform
microstructure transformation in both overlapping and non-
overlapping regions of the wire rod during air cooling, whilepreventing excessive core martensite and network cementite
formation.

2.3.2 Phase Transformation Model. During the cooling
process of hot rolled high carbon steel wire rod, the austeniteundergoes a transformation into pearlite, releasing latent heat
from the phase transformation, thereby causing an increase in
the wire rod’s temperature (Ref 18). Figure 6illustrates the
arrangement of air-cooled conveyor rollers and blowers within
the factory. The Stelmor cooling process in this setup involves
ﬁve conveyor rollers with eight blowers positioned beneaththem. Notably, there is no blower located under the ﬁrst
conveyor roller, resulting in the wire rod being left in the air-
cooled state. The ﬁrst four blowers have a maximum air volumeof 200,000 m
3/h each, while the subsequent four blowers have
a maximum air volume of 168,000 m3/h. This study speciﬁes
that the wire rod must complete its phase transformation beforereaching the 8th blower, ensuring a wire rod temperature ofapproximately 600 /C176C post the 8th blower.
The state of the SWRH82B wire rod during the cooling
process in the actual production is shown in Fig. 7. Initially, the
wire rod exhibits a dark red area at the 5th and 6th blowers,
which then transforms into a bright red hue. Analysis indicates
a phase transformation occurring in the wire rod during the aircooling process, characterized by a temperature increase
leading to the appearance of the bright red coloration. To
replicate this process in simulations accurately, setting aninternal heat source within the wire rod at the onset of the phase
transition is crucial. Finite element simulations of the wire rod
process enable the determination of temperature and coolingrates at different sections of the wire rod as the temperature
decreases. By analyzing the dynamic Continuous CoolingTransformation curve based on experimental data (section 2.2),
the temperature and phase transition initiation times can be
inferred from the cooling rates. Thus, the ﬁnite element
simulation during the phase transformation period (whichvaries with different roller speeds) necessitates the inclusion of
an internal heat source, typically positioned at the 5th and 6th
blowers, with an assigned energy density of 40,000,000 W/m
3.

3.4 Process Optimization
The temperature range for grain boundary cementite pre-
cipitation in SWRH82B steel is 731-783 /C176C. Analysis of the
continuous cooling transformation diagram indicated that acooling rate exceeding 10 /C176C/s leads to minimization of grain
boundary cementite precipitation. Moreover, achieving a cool-
ing rate above 8 /C176C/s before the phase transformation resulted
in a sorbite rate exceeding 90%. To increase the wire rod’s
cooling rate to 10 /C176C/s, adjustments must be made to the airvolume of 2ed, 3rd, and 4th blowers possibly necessitating the
installation of larger frequency blowers. Due to the adverseimpact of speeds exceeding 0.1 m/s on coil shape, adjustmentswere made to the roller conveyor speeds: 0.65 m/s for the two-
roller conveyor, 0.75 m/s for the three-roller conveyor, and
0.7 m/s for the four-roller conveyor. Air volumes for theblowers were set as follows: 150,000 m
3/h for blower 1st,
280,000 m3/h for the 2ed, 3rd, and 4th blowers, and
168,000 m3/h for the 5th, 6th, 7th, and 8th blowers. Analysis
based on the phase transformation temperatures shown in
Table 8reveals that, in a simulation without a heating source,
the phase transformation temperature at 35.3 s is 658.63 /C176C
with a cooling rate of 8 /C176C/s, indicating the heat source for
phase transformation simulation was at 35.3 s.
Figure 14illustrates the temperature variations of the core
and surface of the wire rod in both the lap and non lap areasduring the laying process at 880 /C176C. The temperature difference
between the core and surface was inﬂuenced by the wind speed.
Speciﬁcally, the wind speed between the laying position and thevicinity of the ﬁrst blower (during this stage, no actual blower
exists, but in FLUENT, the convective heat transfer coefﬁcient
is linked to wind speed, so a nominal wind speed is utilized tofacilitate wire rod cooling) was 12.17 m/s. Throughout this
phase, the temperature variance between the core and surface
diminished gradually. Subsequently, the wind speed of the ﬁrstblower increased to 32.18 m/s. This abrupt rise in wind speedresulted in a rapid surface temperature decline, while the core
temperature remained relatively unchanged due to the slower
cooling process relying on internal heat conduction within thematerial. Consequently, the temperature difference between the
core and surface of the wire rod increased during the ﬁrst
blower operation. The wind speed of the second blowerescalated to 60.06 m/s. Comparatively, during the second
blower phase, the temperature difference between the core and
surface of the wire rod continued to widen. The wind speeds ofthe third and fourth blowers mirror that of the second blower.
As the wire rod undergoes constant-speed air cooling, the
temperature gap between the core and surface graduallydiminished. Furthermore, in subsequent cooling stages withreduced wind speed, this temperature difference further
decreased. Simulation outcomes indicated that the cooling rate
of the lap joint pre-phase transition reached 7.79 /C176C /s, while
that of the non lap joint reached 7.95 /C176C /s.

4. Conclusions
In this paper, the microstructure transformation of
SWRH82B steel was studied, and the controlled coolingprocess of wire rod was optimized by numerical simulation to
make the distribution of temperature as well as microstructure
more uniform during the cooling process.
The key ﬁndings are as follows:(1) Setting the opening angle of the Optiﬂex device at 3 /C176
for the ﬁrst four blowers and 1.5 /C176for the subsequent
four blowers resulted in nearly identical surface temper-atures for wire rod sections within and outside the lap
area during air cooling, with errors well within accept-
able limits.
(2) With the increase of the initial roller speed, the tempera-
ture difference of the coil surface in the lap area de-
creased ﬁrst and then increased. The temperaturedifference was minimized when the initial roller con-
veyor speed ranged from 0.70 to 0.75 m/s, with corre-
sponding wire rod spacing between 73.90 and 79.18mm to ensure a more uniform cooling rate within the
lap area.
(3) In optimizing the 880 /C176C laying process, adjusting the
roller speed and increasing the air volume provided byblowers 2-4 to 280,000 m
3/h effectively improved the
content of sorbite and reduced the network cementite.
After thermal simulation experiments, a sorbite rate of92% was achieved, with network cementite below grade
1 and core martensite below grade 0.5.
